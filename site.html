About Baaki

A passbook, not a spreadsheet.

Most money apps ask you to categorize, tag, and forecast before they'll tell you anything useful. Baaki starts from the one number people actually check first: what's left.

The name comes from a word used across Hindi, Urdu, and everyday Tamil-English conversation for exactly that — the balance still owed, or still yours. It's the number a shopkeeper reads off a passbook without a second thought, and the number we think a finance app should lead with too.

Features

Built around one question: what's left?

A handful of things done well, instead of a dashboard full of charts nobody opens twice.

01

Running balance

Every entry — income or spend — updates one live number. No end-of-month surprises about where the money went.

02

Plain-language entries

Log "chai and snacks — ₹80" the way you'd say it out loud. No forced categories or merchant codes.

03

Multiple pots

Split baaki across a spending pot, a savings pot, and a shared pot — each with its own running remainder.

04

Weekly close

A short passbook-style summary every Sunday: in, out, and what's carried forward — nothing more.

05

Shared ledgers

Split rent or trip costs with friends on one shared ledger, so everyone sees the same "who owes what."

06

Offline-first

Entries save locally first and sync when you're back online — built for patchy connections, not just fast ones.

How it works

Three steps, in order

Baaki is deliberately a short, linear flow — there's no setup wizard to get through first.

  1. Log what moved

    Add an entry the moment money comes in or goes out — takes about as long as writing a line in a notebook.

  2. Watch baaki update

    The running balance recalculates instantly, so you always know what's left before you spend again.

  3. Close the week

    Every Sunday, Baaki carries your balance forward into a fresh page — same idea as turning to a new passbook entry.
